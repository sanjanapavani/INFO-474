The Great Tech Correction

Dataset
Tech layoffs 2020–2024 from layoffs.fyi — the most comprehensive public tracker of tech industry layoffs, covering company name, total laid off, percentage of workforce, industry, and date.

Story
Over 500,000 tech workers were laid off between 2020 and 2024, peaking dramatically in January 2023. The title "The Great Tech Correction" frames this as a systemic industry event, not just individual company decisions. The visualization shows both the scale of cuts (total employees) and the brutality of cuts (% of workforce), revealing that smaller companies often suffered more proportionally than giants like Amazon or Google.

Author-driven vs. User-driven
This visualization is primarily author-driven: the story, encoding choices, and annotations are pre-determined to guide the viewer toward a specific insight — that the 2022–2023 period was an unprecedented correction, and that percentage of workforce cut matters as much as raw numbers. However, one level of user interaction is included: clicking a company bar filters the timeline to show only that company's layoff history, allowing viewers to explore individual stories within the broader narrative. This places it in the "martini glass" genre — author-driven structure with a narrow interactive exploration at the end.

Design Principles
Color encodes brutality (% workforce cut) rather than identity — darker red means a higher proportion of the workforce was cut, not just more people. This avoids misleading viewers into thinking Amazon's large absolute number is more "brutal" than Twitter's 75% cut. Visual hierarchy is established through size (large title first), then the animated wave (second attention), then the bar chart (third). Annotations on the peak month and percentage badges add essential context without cluttering.

Clarity, Hierarchy, and Memorability
The dark background with a single red accent color creates strong contrast and a serious editorial tone appropriate to the subject. The animated wave draws the eye first and establishes the temporal story before the viewer reads any text. The "Big cuts vs. Brutal cuts" distinction is the memorable takeaway — viewers leave understanding that scale and severity are two different things.
